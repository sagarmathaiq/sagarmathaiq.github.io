Nepal's Pradesh Structure: White Elephant or Misdiagnosed Problem?

Fiscal Analysis · FCGO CFS 2022/23 · March 2026

← All Projects Who We Are

About This Analysis

Critics call Nepal's seven Pradesh (provincial) governments an unaffordable experiment — a "white elephant" the country cannot sustain. We tested that claim against the government's own audited accounts. The data comes from the Financial Comptroller General Office (FCGO) Consolidated Financial Statement FY 2022/23, the only document that tracks what was actually spent across all three tiers, not what was budgeted.

The findings are not a political position on federalism. They are a factcheck of specific fiscal claims, sourced from primary government data.

Finding 01 · Scale
FCGO CFS 2022/23 Provinces Spend 3.8% of GDP. Not More.

After eliminating intergovernmental transfers to prevent double-counting, the seven provinces together account for just 10.7% of Nepal's total consolidated government expenditure. The 753 local governments — which critics rarely target — spent more than double the combined provincial total.

Finding 02 · Expenditure Composition
FCGO CFS 2022/23 The Federal Govt Is More "Administrative" Than Provinces

The white elephant argument claims provinces burn money on salaries and perks. The audited data says otherwise. The federal government spends 69.76% of its own budget on recurrent items. When sub-national governments are included, the combined figure drops to 57.01% — meaning provinces and locals pull the system toward capital spending, not away from it.

Expenditure Composition: Recurrent vs Capital vs Financial (%)
Key finding: Federal recurrent share (69.76%) exceeds the combined three-tier figure (57.01%) by 12.75 percentage points. Sub-national governments are structurally more capital-oriented than the federal government — the opposite of what critics claim.

Source: FCGO CFS FY 2022/23 — Executive Summary, expenditure composition tables.

Finding 03 · Fiscal Balance
FCGO CFS 2022/23 Provinces Ran a Surplus. The Federal Govt Did Not.

In FY 2022/23, the federal Consolidated Fund recorded a deficit of NPR 181 billion. Both provincial and local tiers reported surpluses — Rs 64B and Rs 78B respectively. The entity creating Nepal's fiscal stress is the center, not the Pradesh tier.

Finding 04 · Capital Expenditure
Nepal MTEF + CPI Data Adding Provinces Did Not Inflate Capital Spending

Before provinces existed, Nepal's capital expenditure benchmark (FY 2017/18) was Rs 271.7B. Adjusting for realized CPI inflation each year creates a counterfactual — what would spending have been without the new tier? The combined federal + provincial actual capex tracks that baseline within a few percent, every single year.

CPI-Adjusted Pre-Federal Baseline vs Actual Federal + Provincial CapEx (Rs Billion)
Five-year average deviation: ±3%. Provinces redistributed existing fiscal capacity — they did not add a net new capital burden. The spending envelope is virtually identical to what Nepal would have spent without a provincial tier.

Source: Nepal MTEF data + realized CPI · MoF Economic Survey 2023/24 / Annapurna Express analysis.

What the Data Does Support
Governance Assessment Legitimate Problems — Real, But Reformable

The numbers don't validate the white elephant claim — but they do surface real and specific governance failures that deserve attention.

  • 📉
    Budget execution is chronically poor
    Federal capital execution: 61.68% in FY 2022/23. Five of seven provinces spent under 50% of their capital budget in FY 2023/24. Under-execution is a system-wide failure — not province-specific. Source: FCGO CFS; Nepal Economic Survey 2024/25.
  • 🏛️
    Political patronage inflates recurrent overhead
    Provinces function as party recruitment pipelines. Ministerial positions and appointees inflate recurrent costs. Gandaki's 2025 restructuring — abolishing 9 offices, merging 32 into 17, saving Rs 400–500M/year — shows this is reformable. Source: Kathmandu Post, Aug 2025.
  • 💰
    Own-source revenue is structurally thin
    All 7 provinces raised only Rs 112B internally vs Rs 204B total expenditure in FY 2022/23. The cause is constitutional design: the center retained customs, corporate tax, and most VAT while provinces received vehicle tax and entertainment levies. Source: FCGO CFS 2022/23.
  • 🔁
    Functional duplication — created by the center
    The federal government still maintains parallel field offices for agriculture, livestock, and education in provinces — functions the constitution assigned downward. The duplication cost is real but caused by the center's unwillingness to transfer, not by provincial existence. Source: Review of Democracy, 2025.

Conclusion

The White Elephant Is Not Where Critics Are Looking

Seven provinces account for under 11% of total government spending, produce no net increase in capital expenditure compared to the pre-federal baseline, and run fiscal surpluses while the federal government runs deficits. Their expenditure structure is more development-oriented than the center's.

The real problems — patronage appointments, thin own-source revenue, poor budget execution — are genuine but describe reformable governance failures present at all tiers, especially the federal one. Gandaki's 2025 administrative restructuring demonstrated provinces can self-reform.

If the fiscal "white elephant" label belongs anywhere, the FCGO numbers point to the federal government's own recurrent bloat: 69.76% recurrent spending, Rs 181B deficit, 42.66% debt-to-GDP — not a Pradesh tier spending 3.8 paisa of every rupee of national income.
