About SagarmathaIQ

SagarmathaIQ is a quantitative research initiative focused on election forecasting in Nepal. Our team consists of Nepali professionals with PhDs in physics and mathematics, currently working in statistical modeling and data science at multinational companies.

We build probabilistic forecasting models using established statistical techniques adapted to Nepal's unique electoral context. Rather than making single-number predictions, we generate thousands of simulated elections to show the range of possible outcomes and their relative likelihoods.

Named after Mount Everest (Sagarmatha in Nepali), our mission is to provide transparent, data-driven election analysis that honestly represents uncertainty rather than projecting false confidence. We believe good forecasting acknowledges what we don't know as clearly as what we do.

Research Methodology

Our forecasting approach recognizes that each party has a reliable base of support plus additional votes that depend on the political mood. We model this by dividing votes into two parts: (1) a guaranteed minimum based on each party's current organizational strength, and (2) contested votes that shift between parties based on national sentiment.

A key challenge is accounting for new parties like RSP. In 2079, RSP was just starting out with minimal organization. But by 2082, they've built a real nationwide presence. Simply using their 2079 numbers would underestimate their current strength. We account for this growth by setting their baseline higher—around 20% nationally—based on their youth support and urban expansion since 2079.

The model tracks national political trends—like anti-establishment sentiment and ideological preferences—and applies them consistently across both the proportional (PR) and direct election (FPTP) systems. This ensures our forecasts for both parts of parliament are realistic and internally consistent.

Key Forecasting Challenges

The 2082 parliamentary elections present several unique challenges:

  • New party growth: RSP's 2079 results don't reflect their 2082 strength. They've grown significantly in organization and support, so we can't just use old numbers.
  • Multi-party races: Every constituency has multiple competitive candidates from different parties. The model simulates all major parties competing in each race rather than limiting to just two candidates.
  • Changed political climate: The GenZ uprising shifted the baseline political mood. There's clearly elevated anti-establishment sentiment compared to pre-uprising norms.
  • Two election systems: Nepal elects parliament through both proportional voting (PR) and direct constituency races (FPTP). The same national trends affect both, so we model them together rather than separately.

Accounting for all parties in every constituency is important for accuracy. RSP is now genuinely competitive in many districts where they might have been overlooked using simpler approaches.

How the Model Works

The forecasting model combines historical voting patterns with current political realities:

  • Party strength baselines: Each party starts with a minimum support level reflecting their actual 2082 organizational presence, not just 2079 results
  • National trends: We track overall political sentiment (anti-establishment feelings, ideological shifts) and apply these consistently across all races
  • Local competition: Direct elections (FPTP) account for all major parties competing in each constituency, not just the previous winner and runner-up
  • Uncertainty modeling: Rather than single predictions, we run thousands of simulated elections to show the range of possible outcomes

Our forecasts were refined using detailed analysis of specific races. For example, examining the Jhapa-5 contest helped us better calibrate how much stronger RSP has become since 2079 and how post-GenZ sentiment affects the baseline political environment.

All results shown in our dashboards come from 10,000 simulated elections. This approach lets us present probabilities (like "UML has an 87% chance of winning the most seats") rather than false precision ("UML will win exactly 84 seats").

Transparency and Limitations

Our forecasts involve several sources of uncertainty that readers should understand:

  • Limited polling data: Nepal lacks high-quality public polling, so our party strength estimates rely on political analysis and historical patterns rather than direct survey data
  • Expert judgment: Decisions about how much parties have grown since 2079 incorporate assessments from political observers and ground reporting
  • Simplified model: Real politics is complex. Our model captures major trends (anti-establishment sentiment, party strength) but can't account for every local factor
  • Turnout assumptions: We assume 65% turnout based on 2079 patterns. Actual turnout could differ and change competitive dynamics
  • Independent races: The model treats each constituency as somewhat independent, which might miss coordinated regional swings

We validate our approach by comparing forecasts against detailed political reporting from specific constituencies. For example, analysis of the Jhapa-5 race helped confirm and refine our assumptions about RSP's growth and current political sentiment.

All forecasts are presented as probabilities and ranges (95% confidence intervals), never as exact predictions. When we say "UML has 87% chance of winning most seats," we're showing that UML led in 87 out of 100 simulated elections—not claiming certainty about the outcome.
